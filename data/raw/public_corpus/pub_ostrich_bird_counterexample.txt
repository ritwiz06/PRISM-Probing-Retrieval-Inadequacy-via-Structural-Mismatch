<p><b>Ostriches</b> are large <a href="/wiki/Flightless_bird" title="Flightless bird">flightless</a> <a href="/wiki/Bird" title="Bird">birds</a>. Two living species are recognised: the <a href="/wiki/Common_ostrich" title="Common ostrich">common ostrich</a>, native to large parts of <a href="/wiki/Sub-Saharan_Africa" title="Sub-Saharan Africa">Sub-Saharan Africa</a>, and the <a href="/wiki/Somali_ostrich" title="Somali ostrich">Somali ostrich</a>, native to the <a href="/wiki/Horn_of_Africa" title="Horn of Africa">Horn of Africa</a>.
</p><p>They are the heaviest and largest living birds, with adult common ostriches weighing anywhere between 63.5–145&#160;kg (140–320&#160;lb), and laying the largest <a href="/wiki/Egg" title="Egg">eggs</a> of any living land animal.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-world&#95;3-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-world-3"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>3<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> With the ability to run at 70&#160;km/h (43&#160;mph),<sup id="cite&#95;ref-Doherty&#95;4-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Doherty-4"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>4<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> they are the fastest birds on land. They are farmed worldwide, with significant industries <a href="/wiki/Ostrich_farming_in_the_Philippines" title="Ostrich farming in the Philippines">in the Philippines</a> and <a href="/wiki/Ostrich_farming_in_Namibia" title="Ostrich farming in Namibia">in Namibia</a>. <a href="/wiki/South_Africa" title="South Africa">South Africa</a> produces about 70% of global ostrich products,<sup id="cite&#95;ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>5<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> with the industry largely centered around the town of <a href="/wiki/Oudtshoorn" title="Oudtshoorn">Oudtshoorn</a>. <a href="/wiki/Ostrich_leather" title="Ostrich leather">Ostrich leather</a> is a lucrative commodity, and the large feathers are used as <a href="/wiki/Plume_(feather)" title="Plume (feather)">plumes</a> for the decoration of ceremonial headgear. <a href="/wiki/Ostrich_egg" title="Ostrich egg">Ostrich eggs</a> and <a href="/wiki/Ostrich_meat" title="Ostrich meat">meat</a> have been used by humans for millennia. <a href="/wiki/Ostrich_oil" title="Ostrich oil">Ostrich oil</a> is another product that is made using ostrich fat. 
</p><p>Ostriches belong to the <a href="/wiki/Genus" title="Genus">genus</a> <i><b>Struthio</b></i> in the order <a href="/wiki/Struthioniformes" title="Struthioniformes">Struthioniformes</a>, part of the infra-class <a href="/wiki/Palaeognathae" title="Palaeognathae">Palaeognathae</a>, a diverse group of flightless and weakly-flying birds also known as <a href="/wiki/Ratite" title="Ratite">ratites</a> that includes the <a href="/wiki/Cassowary" title="Cassowary">cassowaries</a>, <a href="/wiki/Emu" title="Emu">emus</a>, <a href="/wiki/Rhea_(bird)" title="Rhea (bird)">rheas</a>, <a href="/wiki/Kiwi_(bird)" title="Kiwi (bird)">kiwi</a>, <a href="/wiki/Tinamou" title="Tinamou">tinamous</a>, and the extinct <a href="/wiki/Elephant_bird" title="Elephant bird">elephant birds</a> and <a href="/wiki/Moa" title="Moa">moa</a>.
</p><p>The common ostrich was historically native to the <a href="/wiki/Arabian_Peninsula" title="Arabian Peninsula">Arabian Peninsula</a>, and ostriches were present across Asia as far east as China and Mongolia during the <a href="/wiki/Late_Pleistocene" title="Late Pleistocene">Late Pleistocene</a> and possibly into the <a href="/wiki/Holocene_climatic_optimum" title="Holocene climatic optimum">Holocene</a>.
</p>

<div class="mw-heading mw-heading2"><h2 id="Taxonomic_history">Taxonomic history</h2></div>
<p>The genus <i>Struthio</i> was first described by <a href="/wiki/Carl_Linnaeus" title="Carl Linnaeus">Carl Linnaeus</a> in 1758. The genus was used by Linnaeus and other early taxonomists to include the <a href="/wiki/Emu" title="Emu">emu</a>, <a href="/wiki/Rhea_(bird)" title="Rhea (bird)">rhea</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Cassowary" title="Cassowary">cassowary</a>, until they each were placed in their own genera.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-Gray&#95;1-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Gray-1"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>1<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> The Somali ostrich (<i>Struthio molybdophanes</i>) has recently become recognised as a separate species by most authorities, while others are still reviewing the evidence.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>6<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup><sup id="cite&#95;ref-BLI&#95;7-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-BLI-7"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>7<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>
</p>
<div class="mw-heading mw-heading2"><h2 id="Evolution">Evolution</h2></div>
<p><a href="/wiki/Struthionidae" title="Struthionidae">Struthionidae</a> is a member of the <a href="/wiki/Struthioniformes" title="Struthioniformes">Struthioniformes</a>, a group of <a href="/wiki/Paleognath" class="mw-redirect" title="Paleognath">paleognath</a> birds which first appeared during the Early <a href="/wiki/Eocene" title="Eocene">Eocene</a>, and includes a variety of flightless forms which were present across the Northern Hemisphere (Europe, Asia and North America) during the Eocene epoch. The closest relatives of Struthionidae within the Struthioniformes are the <a href="/wiki/Ergilornithidae" class="mw-redirect" title="Ergilornithidae">Ergilornithidae</a>, known from the late Eocene to early <a href="/wiki/Pliocene" title="Pliocene">Pliocene</a> of Asia. It is therefore most likely that Struthionidae originated in Asia.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-:0&#95;8-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:0-8"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>8<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>
</p><p>The earliest fossils of the genus <i>Struthio</i> are from the early <a href="/wiki/Miocene" title="Miocene">Miocene</a> ~21 million years ago of Namibia in Africa, so it is proposed that genus is of African origin. By the middle to late <a href="/wiki/Miocene" title="Miocene">Miocene</a> (5–13 mya) they had spread to and become widespread across Eurasia.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>9<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> While the relationship of the African fossil species is comparatively straightforward, many Asian species of ostrich have been described from fragmentary remains, and their interrelationships and how they relate to the African ostriches are confusing. In India, Mongolia and <a href="/wiki/China" title="China">China</a>, ostriches are known to have become extinct only around, or even after, the end of the <a href="/wiki/Last_glacial_period" class="mw-redirect" title="Last glacial period">last ice age</a>; images of ostriches have been found prehistoric Chinese pottery and <a href="/wiki/Petroglyph" title="Petroglyph">petroglyphs</a>.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>10<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup><sup id="cite&#95;ref-janz&#95;11-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-janz-11"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>11<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup><sup id="cite&#95;ref-r1&#95;12-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-r1-12"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>12<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup><sup id="cite&#95;ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>13<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>
</p>

<div class="mw-heading mw-heading2"><h2 id="Distribution_and_habitat">Distribution and habitat</h2></div>
<p>Today, ostriches are only found naturally in the wild in <a href="/wiki/Africa" title="Africa">Africa</a>, where they occur in a range of open arid and semi-arid habitats such as <a href="/wiki/Savanna" title="Savanna">savannas</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Sahel" title="Sahel">Sahel</a>, both north and south of the equatorial forest zone.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-Donegan&#95;2002&#95;14-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Donegan_2002-14"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>14<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/Somali_ostrich" title="Somali ostrich">Somali ostrich</a> occurs in the <a href="/wiki/Horn_of_Africa" title="Horn of Africa">Horn of Africa</a>, having evolved isolated from the common ostrich by the geographic barrier of the <a href="/wiki/East_African_Rift" title="East African Rift">East African Rift</a>. In some areas, the common ostrich's <a href="/wiki/Masai_ostrich" title="Masai ostrich">Masai subspecies</a> occurs alongside the Somali ostrich, but they are kept from interbreeding by behavioural and ecological differences.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-Freitag&#95;15-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Freitag-15"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>15<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/Arabian_ostrich" title="Arabian ostrich">Arabian ostriches</a> in <a href="/wiki/Asia_Minor" class="mw-redirect" title="Asia Minor">Asia Minor</a> and <a href="/wiki/Arabia" class="mw-redirect" title="Arabia">Arabia</a> were hunted to extinction by the middle of the 20th century, and in <a href="/wiki/Israel" title="Israel">Israel</a> attempts to introduce <a href="/wiki/North_African_ostrich" title="North African ostrich">North African ostriches</a> to fill their ecological role have failed.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>16<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> Escaped common ostriches in Australia have established <a href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/feral" class="extiw" title="wiktionary:feral">feral</a> populations.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>17<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup><sup id="cite&#95;ref-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-18"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>18<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup><sup id="cite&#95;ref-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-19"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>19<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>
</p>
<div class="mw-heading mw-heading2"><h2 id="Diet">Diet</h2></div>
<p>Ostriches are omnivores although plants make up the majority of their diet. They primarily eat plant foods including leafy greens, roots, grasses, fruit and succulents. They will also eat small animals such as lizards, frogs and mice, as well as insects such as grasshoppers or locusts<sup id="cite&#95;ref-20" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-20"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>20<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>. 
</p><p>Ostriches do not have teeth, and like other birds, swallow sand and pebbles to grind food in their stomachs.
</p>
<div class="mw-heading mw-heading2"><h2 id="Species">Species</h2></div>
<figure class="mw-default-size" typeof="mw:File/Thumb"><a href="/wiki/File:Somali_ostrich.jpg" class="mw-file-description"><img src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e1/Somali_ostrich.jpg/250px-Somali_ostrich.jpg" decoding="async" width="250" height="170" class="mw-file-element" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e1/Somali_ostrich.jpg/500px-Somali_ostrich.jpg 2x" data-file-width="1000" data-file-height="680" /></a><figcaption>A male Somali ostrich in a <a href="/wiki/Kenya" title="Kenya">Kenyan</a> <a href="/wiki/Savanna" title="Savanna">savanna</a>, showing its blueish neck</figcaption></figure>
<p>In 2008, <i>S. linxiaensis</i> was transferred to the genus <i><a href="/wiki/Orientornis" title="Orientornis">Orientornis</a>.</i><sup id="cite&#95;ref-21" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-21"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>21<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> Three additional species, <i>S. pannonicus</i>, <i>S. dmanisensis</i>, and <i>S. transcaucasicus</i>, were transferred to the genus <i><a href="/wiki/Pachystruthio" title="Pachystruthio">Pachystruthio</a></i> in 2019.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-Zelenkov2019&#95;22-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Zelenkov2019-22"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>22<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> Several additional fossil forms are <a href="/wiki/Ichnotaxa" class="mw-redirect" title="Ichnotaxa">ichnotaxa</a> (that is, classified according to the organism's <a href="/wiki/Trace_fossil" title="Trace fossil">trace fossils</a> such as footprints rather than its body) and their association with those described from distinctive bones is contentious and in need of revision pending more good material.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-Bibi&#95;23-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Bibi-23"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>23<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>
</p><p>The species are:
</p>
<ul><li>Prehistoric
