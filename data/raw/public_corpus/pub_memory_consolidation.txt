</div><div class="mw-content-ltr mw-parser-output" lang="en" dir="ltr"><div class="shortdescription nomobile noexcerpt noprint searchaux" style="display:none">Category of memory stabilizing processes</div>
<figure typeof="mw:File/Thumb"><a href="/wiki/File:Memory_consolidation.svg" class="mw-file-description"><img alt="position of consolidation to the information-to-memory process" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/1/1f/Memory_consolidation.svg/500px-Memory_consolidation.svg.png" decoding="async" width="345" height="99" class="mw-file-element" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/1/1f/Memory_consolidation.svg/960px-Memory_consolidation.svg.png 2x" data-file-width="737" data-file-height="212" /></a><figcaption>The line processes to make information memory</figcaption></figure><p><b>Memory consolidation</b> is a process in the brain that stabilizes newly learned information, allowing the memory to be stored long-term.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-Dudai&#95;1-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Dudai-1"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>1<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>  A memory trace is the biological change in neural circuits that occurs during learning. 
</p><p>Consolidation is divided into two main processes, synaptic consolidation and systems consolidation. <b>Synaptic consolidation</b> occurs rapidly on a small scale in the individual synapses within the first few hours of learning. <b>Systems consolidation</b> occurs on a larger scale and involves gradual reorganization and reduced reliance on the hippocampus and increased involvement of cortical networks over a period of weeks to years. 
</p><p>These processes involve molecular and cellular changes that stabilize neural representations, referred to as a <a href="/wiki/Neuronal_ensemble" title="Neuronal ensemble">neural ensemble</a> (<a href="/wiki/Engram_(neuropsychology)" title="Engram (neuropsychology)">engram</a>).<sup id="cite&#95;ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>2<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> 
</p><p>A third process, <b>reconsolidation</b>, has been proposed, in which previously consolidated memories can become unstable again after retrieval and may need to be updated before being re-stabilized.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-Nader&#95;3-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Nader-3"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>3<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>
</p>
<meta property="mw:PageProp/toc" />
<div class="mw-heading mw-heading2"><h2 id="History">History</h2><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Memory_consolidation&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></div><p>
Memory consolidation was first referred to in the writings of the renowned <a href="/wiki/Ancient_Rome" title="Ancient Rome">Roman</a> teacher of <a href="/wiki/Rhetoric" title="Rhetoric">rhetoric</a> <a href="/wiki/Quintillian" class="mw-redirect" title="Quintillian">Quintillian</a>, who noted that memory improves over time.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-Dudai&#95;1-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Dudai-1"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>1<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> In the late 19th century, German researchers <a href="/wiki/Georg_Elias_M%C3%BCller" title="Georg Elias Müller">Müller</a> and Alfons Pilzecker formally introduced the idea of consolidation, proposing memory takes time to fixate and stablize.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-Dudai&#95;1-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Dudai-1"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>1<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> </p><figure typeof="mw:File/Thumb"><a href="/wiki/File:Gray739-emphasizing-hippocampus.png" class="mw-file-description"><img src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/2e/Gray739-emphasizing-hippocampus.png/330px-Gray739-emphasizing-hippocampus.png" decoding="async" width="309" height="234" class="mw-file-element" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/2/2e/Gray739-emphasizing-hippocampus.png 2x" data-file-width="500" data-file-height="379" /></a><figcaption>Lateral view of the hippocampus which is located in the medial temporal lobe</figcaption></figure><p>Systematic studies of <a href="/wiki/Anterograde_amnesia" title="Anterograde amnesia">anterograde amnesia</a> started to emerge in the 1960s and 1970s. The case of <a href="/wiki/Henry_Molaison" title="Henry Molaison">Henry Molaison</a>, formerly known as patient H.M., became a <a href="/wiki/Landmark" title="Landmark">landmark</a> in studies of memory as it relates to <a href="/wiki/Amnesia" title="Amnesia">amnesia</a> and the removal of the <a href="/wiki/Hippocampus" title="Hippocampus">hippocampal zone</a> and sparked massive interest in the study of <a href="/wiki/Lesions" class="mw-redirect" title="Lesions">brain lesions</a> and their effect on memory. Research into other patients with resections of the MTL have shown a positive relationship between the degree of memory impairment and the extent of MTL removal which points to a temporal <a href="/wiki/Gradient" title="Gradient">gradient</a> in the consolidating nature of the MTL.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-ScovilleMilner&#95;4-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ScovilleMilner-4"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>4<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> These findings helped establish the modern theories of memory consolidation and its underlying neural mechanisms.
</p><div class="mw-heading mw-heading2"><h2 id="Synaptic_consolidation">Synaptic consolidation</h2><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Memory_consolidation&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Synaptic consolidation"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></div>
<p><a href="/wiki/Synapse" title="Synapse">Synaptic consolidation</a> is the earlier phase of memory consolidation in which newly formed memories are stabilized by changes occurring at individual synapses. This process occurs within minutes to hours after learning and is thought to support long-term memory formation. <a href="/wiki/Long-term_potentiation#Late_phase" title="Long-term potentiation">Late-phase LTP</a> is the long-lasting increase in synaptic strength following repeated stimulation and is thought to be the cellular process underlying synaptic consolidation.
</p>
<div class="mw-heading mw-heading3"><h3 id="Standard_model">Standard model</h3><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Memory_consolidation&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Standard model"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></div>
<p>The standard model of synaptic consolidation suggests that learning activates signaling pathways inside the neurons which change <a href="/wiki/Gene_expression" title="Gene expression">gene expression</a> and <a href="/wiki/Protein_synthesis" class="mw-redirect" title="Protein synthesis">protein synthesis</a>. These changes result in lasting alteration of synaptic proteins, as well as synaptic remodeling and growth. Immediately after learning, these alterations are vulnerable to disruptions which is why newly formed memories are thought to be fragile initially.  Disruptions caused by specific drugs, antibodies and gross physical trauma can block the effects of synaptic consolidation.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-Dudai&#95;1-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Dudai-1"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>1<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>
</p>
<div class="mw-heading mw-heading3"><h3 id="Synaptic_Plasticity">Synaptic Plasticity</h3><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Memory_consolidation&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Synaptic Plasticity"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></div>
<p>Synaptic consolidation is supported by multiple forms of <a href="/wiki/Synaptic_plasticity" title="Synaptic plasticity">synaptic plasticity</a>. Synaptic plasticity is largely defined as the ability of a neural connection to change in strength over time due to exposure to different levels of stimuli.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>5<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> Two major forms of synaptic plasticity include <a href="/wiki/Long-term_potentiation" title="Long-term potentiation">long-term potentiation</a> which strengthens synaptic connections and <a href="/wiki/Long-term_depression" title="Long-term depression">long-term depression</a> which weakens synaptic connections.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-:1&#95;6-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:1-6"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>6<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> 
</p>
<div class="mw-heading mw-heading3"><h3 id="Long-term_potentiation">Long-term potentiation</h3><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Memory_consolidation&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Long-term potentiation"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></div>
<style data-mw-deduplicate="TemplateStyles:r1320445320">.mw-parser-output .hatnote{font-style:italic}.mw-parser-output div.hatnote{padding-left:1.6em;margin-bottom:0.5em}.mw-parser-output .hatnote i{font-style:normal}.mw-parser-output .hatnote+link+.hatnote{margin-top:-0.5em}@media print{body.ns-0 .mw-parser-output .hatnote{display:none!important}}</style><div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Long-term_potentiation" title="Long-term potentiation">Long-term potentiation</a></div>
<p>LTP is the prolonged strengthening of <a href="/wiki/Synaptic_transmission" class="mw-redirect" title="Synaptic transmission">synaptic transmission</a><sup id="cite&#95;ref-Tronson&#95;7-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Tronson-7"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>7<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> following repeated stimulation. LTP is widely considered a key mechanism in memory and learning because of its ability to strengthen communication between neurons.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-:1&#95;6-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:1-6"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>6<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup><sup id="cite&#95;ref-:2&#95;8-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:2-8"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>8<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> 
</p>
<div class="mw-heading mw-heading3"><h3 id="Structural_Plasticity">Structural Plasticity</h3><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Memory_consolidation&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Structural Plasticity"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></div>
<p>Structural plasticity refers to large-scale changes in brain structure, encompassing changes in neuron shape, supporting cell shape, and formation of new cells.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>9<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> Because memory consolidation involves the long-term stabilization of <a href="/wiki/Neural_circuit" title="Neural circuit">neural circuits</a>, these large-scale structural changes are thought to contribute to the persistence and organization of consolidated memories.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-:2&#95;8-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:2-8"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>8<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> The following is a brief list of some of the most significant mechanisms of plasticity in the brain.
</p>
<div class="mw-heading mw-heading4"><h4 id="Neurogenesis">Neurogenesis</h4><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Memory_consolidation&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Neurogenesis"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></div>
<link rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style" href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r1320445320" /><div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Neurogenesis" title="Neurogenesis">Neurogenesis</a></div>
<p><a href="/wiki/Neurogenesis" title="Neurogenesis">Neurogenesis</a> is the process of making new neurons. It is most prominent during development but it has been proposed that newly generated neurons in the hippocampus may support certain forms of memory consolidation—particularly those involving long-term retention.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>10<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>
</p>
<div class="mw-heading mw-heading4"><h4 id="Synaptic_Remodeling">Synaptic Remodeling</h4><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Memory_consolidation&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Synaptic Remodeling"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></div>
<p><a href="/wiki/Dendritic_spine" title="Dendritic spine">Dendritic spine</a> remodeling is a key mechanism of structural plasticity. Alterations in the number and shape of dendritic spines have been associated with memory and learning and are believed to play a key role in converting transient synaptic modifications into long-lasting memory traces.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>11<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>
</p><p><a href="/wiki/Synaptogenesis" title="Synaptogenesis">Synaptogenesis</a> and <a href="/wiki/Synaptic_pruning" title="Synaptic pruning">synaptic pruning</a> also play a role in structural plasticity. Synaptogenesis is the process by which two neurons form a new synapse,<sup id="cite&#95;ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>12<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> and synaptic pruning is the process by which a synaptic connection between two neurons is eliminated.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>13<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> This allows for a high level of variability in neural pathways as they can be subject to change based on different conditions. By acting together as a mechanism that mediates overall synaptic changes, synaptogenesis and pruning can modify how information is routed and stored over time, thereby influencing the long-term consolidation of memories.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>14<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>
</p>
<div class="mw-heading mw-heading4"><h4 id="Myelination">Myelination</h4><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Memory_consolidation&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Myelination"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></div>
<link rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style" href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r1320445320" /><div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Myelination" title="Myelination">Myelination</a></div>
<p>Myelination is the process with which neuronal axons are insulated by myelin which can also change over time. Current studies have established that myelin has a high rate of change in the brain, allowing the brain to optimize functioning speeds based on particular needs.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>15<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> Systems-level memory consolidation depends on coordinated communication across multiple brain regions, thus, activity-dependent changes in myelination are thought to improve the timing and efficiency of these networks, contributing to some of the long-term stabilization of memories.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>16<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup><sup id="cite&#95;ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>17<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>
</p>
<div class="mw-heading mw-heading3"><h3 id="Spacing_effect">Spacing effect</h3><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Memory_consolidation&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Spacing effect"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></div>
<link rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style" href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r1320445320" /><div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Spacing_effect" title="Spacing effect">Spacing effect</a></div>
<p>The spacing effect refers to the idea that information is better retained when the learning is distributed over time rather than <a href="/wiki/Cramming_(education)" title="Cramming (education)">cramming</a> into a single session. This is thought to enhance memory consolidation as it allows for more time for synaptic and molecular processes such as protein synthesis to occur between the learning sessions and thereby strengthen long-term memory.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-Litman&#95;18-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Litman-18"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>18<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup><sup id="cite&#95;ref-Dudai&#95;et&#95;al.&#95;2015&#95;19-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Dudai_et_al._2015-19"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>19<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>
</p>
<div class="mw-heading mw-heading3"><h3 id="Protein_synthesis">Protein synthesis</h3><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Memory_consolidation&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Protein synthesis"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></div>
<p><a href="/wiki/Protein_synthesis" class="mw-redirect" title="Protein synthesis">Protein synthesis</a> has shown to play an important role in the formation of new <a href="/wiki/Memories" class="mw-redirect" title="Memories">memories</a>. Studies have shown that <a href="/wiki/Protein_synthesis_inhibitor" title="Protein synthesis inhibitor">protein synthesis inhibitors</a> administered after <a href="/wiki/Learning" title="Learning">learning</a> weaken memory, suggesting that protein synthesis is required for memory consolidation. Additionally, reports have suggested that the effects of protein synthesis inhibitors also inhibit <a href="/wiki/Long-term_potentiation" title="Long-term potentiation">LTP</a>.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-Gold&#95;20-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Gold-20"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>20<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> However, other results have shown that protein synthesis may not in fact be necessary for memory consolidation, as it has been found that the formation of memories can withstand vast amounts of <a href="/wiki/Protein_synthesis_inhibitor" title="Protein synthesis inhibitor">protein synthesis inhibition</a>, suggesting that its role may not be absolute.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-Gold&#95;20-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Gold-20"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>20<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>
</p>
<div class="mw-heading mw-heading2"><h2 id="Systems_consolidation">Systems consolidation</h2><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Memory_consolidation&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Systems consolidation"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></div>
<p>Systems consolidation is the later phase of <a href="/wiki/Memory" title="Memory">memory</a> consolidation in which memories are reorganized across brain regions over time. Newly formed memories are first encoded in the hippocampus and then are gradually reorganized across cortical networks in a more permanent form of storage.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-Dudai&#95;et&#95;al.&#95;2015&#95;19-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Dudai_et_al._2015-19"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>19<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup><sup id="cite&#95;ref-Roediger&#95;21-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Roediger-21"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>21<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> Systems consolidation is a slow dynamic process which occurs over days to years as a gradual shift from hippocampus-dependent to hippocampus-independent memory storage.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-Roediger&#95;21-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Roediger-21"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>21<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup><sup id="cite&#95;ref-Dudai&#95;et&#95;al.&#95;2015&#95;19-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Dudai_et_al._2015-19"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>19<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup><sup id="cite&#95;ref-Frankland&#95;22-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Frankland-22"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>22<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>
</p><p>Systems consolidation is proposed to be involved in repeated reactivation of memory traces especially during sleep. The reactivations help strengthen the connections between cortical regions thereby gradually reducing reliance on the hippocampus. Over time, this process supports stabilization and long-term memory storage.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-Dudai&#95;et&#95;al.&#95;2015&#95;19-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Dudai_et_al._2015-19"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>19<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup><sup id="cite&#95;ref-Frankland&#95;22-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Frankland-22"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>22<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>
</p>
<div class="mw-heading mw-heading3"><h3 id="Standard_model_2">Standard model</h3><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Memory_consolidation&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Standard model"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></div>
<p>The standard model of systems consolidation, proposed by Squire and Alvarez (1995)<sup id="cite&#95;ref-Squire&#95;1995&#95;23-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Squire_1995-23"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>23<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>, suggests that newly acquired memories are initially encoded in the hippocampus and cortical regions. In this model, the hippocampus temporarily stores memories because the synapses are able to change quickly whereas the neocortical synapses change more slowly over time.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-Squire&#95;1995&#95;23-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Squire_1995-23"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>23<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> Over time, the repeated reactivation strengthens the cortico-cortical connections and therefore reduces reliance on the hippocampus. This leads to long-term memories becoming increasingly supported by neocortical networks.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-JamesLMcGaugh&#95;24-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-JamesLMcGaugh-24"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>24<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup><sup id="cite&#95;ref-Nader&#95;3-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Nader-3"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>3<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> 
</p>
<div class="mw-heading mw-heading3"><h3 id="Multiple_trace_theory">Multiple trace theory</h3><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Memory_consolidation&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Multiple trace theory"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></div>
<p>Multiple trace theory (MTT) proposes that the hippocampus remains involved in the storage and retrieval of episodic memories regardless of age.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-NadelMoscovitch&#95;25-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-NadelMoscovitch-25"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>25<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> MTT argues that episodic memories continue to rely on hippocampal networks. In contrast, the standard model suggests memories become independent of the hippocampus over time. However, semantic memories can be established in structures apart from the hippocampal system, such as the <a href="/wiki/Neocortex" title="Neocortex">neocortex</a>, in the process of consolidation.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-NadelMoscovitch&#95;25-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-NadelMoscovitch-25"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>25<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> 
</p>
<div class="mw-heading mw-heading4"><h4 id="Criticisms">Criticisms</h4><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Memory_consolidation&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: Criticisms"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></div>
<p>Haist, Gore, and Mao, sought to examine the temporal nature of consolidation within the hippocampus to test MTT against the standard view.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-Haist&#95;26-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Haist-26"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>26<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> They found that the hippocampus does not substantially contribute to the recollection of remote memories after a period of a few years. 
</p>
<div class="mw-heading mw-heading3"><h3 id="Semantic_vs._episodic_memory">Semantic vs. episodic memory</h3><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Memory_consolidation&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16" title="Edit section: Semantic vs. episodic memory"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></div>
<p>Nadel and Moscovitch argued <a href="/wiki/Semantic_memory" title="Semantic memory">semantic memory</a> and <a href="/wiki/Episodic_memory" title="Episodic memory">episodic memory</a> need to be distinguished as relying on two different memory systems. Amnesic patients with hippocampal damage show traces of memories and this has been used as support for the standard model because it suggests that memories are retained apart from the hippocampal system.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-NadelMoscovitch&#95;25-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-NadelMoscovitch-25"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>25<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> Nadel and Moscovitch argue that these retained memories have lost the richness of experience and exist as depersonalized events that have been semanticized over time.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-NadelMoscovitch&#95;25-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-NadelMoscovitch-25"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>25<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> They suggest that this instead provides support for their notion that episodic memories rely significantly on the hippocampal system but semantic memories can be established elsewhere in the brain and survive hippocampal damage.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-NadelMoscovitch&#95;25-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-NadelMoscovitch-25"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>25<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>
</p>
<div class="mw-heading mw-heading3"><h3 id="Sleep_consolidation">Sleep consolidation</h3><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Memory_consolidation&amp;action=edit&amp;section=17" title="Edit section: Sleep consolidation"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></div>
<link rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style" href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r1320445320" /><div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">See also: <a href="/wiki/Sleep_and_memory" title="Sleep and memory">Sleep and memory</a></div>
<p>Sleep has been proposed to play a role in systems consolidation as it is thought to support long-term stabilization of memories. During sleep, newly encoded memories are stabilized and reorganized with the help of the interactions between the neocortex and the hippocampus.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-27" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-27"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>27<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>
</p>
<div class="mw-heading mw-heading2"><h2 id="Reconsolidation">Reconsolidation</h2><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Memory_consolidation&amp;action=edit&amp;section=18" title="Edit section: Reconsolidation"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></div>
<p>Memory reconsolidation is the process by which previously consolidated memories become unstable after retrieval and must be re-stabilized to persist.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-Tronson&#95;7-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Tronson-7"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>7<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> It is a distinct process that allows existing memories to be maintained, strengthened and modified over time. Reconsolidation is considered different from initial consolidation, despite its overlap in function (e.g. <a href="/wiki/Storage_(memory)" title="Storage (memory)">storage</a>) and its mechanisms (e.g. <a href="/wiki/Protein_synthesis" class="mw-redirect" title="Protein synthesis">protein synthesis</a>). While widely supported, reconsolidation is an area of ongoing research and debate regarding mechanisms and functional significance. Furthermore, studies have shown that when reconsolidation is disrupted, memory expression can be altered, particularly in fear-related learning paradigms. 
</p>
<div class="mw-heading mw-heading4"><h4 id="Criticisms_2">Criticisms</h4><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Memory_consolidation&amp;action=edit&amp;section=19" title="Edit section: Criticisms"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></div>
<p>Some studies have supported the reconsolidation theory, while others have failed to demonstrate disruption of consolidated memory after retrieval. These inconsistencies suggest that reconsolidation may depend on specific conditions and thus remains an area of ongoing debate.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-Tronson&#95;7-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Tronson-7"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>7<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> 
</p>
